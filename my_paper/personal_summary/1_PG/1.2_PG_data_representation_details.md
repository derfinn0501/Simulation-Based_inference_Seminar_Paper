# Data Representation Details

## Intuition

Simulator outputs can be high-dimensional, such as images, long time series, or many summary measurements, so representing $x$ well is central to SBI performance (Guide, p7:L41-L46).

Handcrafted summary statistics $S(x)$ reduce dimension and can focus inference on scientifically meaningful properties, especially when the simulator is expected to capture only part of the real data (Guide, p7:L47-L53; p8:L1-L3).

The ideal summary keeps all parameter-relevant information, so inference from $S(x)$ is close to inference from raw data $x$; for complex nonlinear simulators, sufficient statistics are usually unknown (Guide, p8:L2-L10).

Embedding networks learn summaries directly from simulated data and can be trained end-to-end with the SBI objective, which avoids a separate manual feature-design step (Guide, p8:L11-L19).

Architecture should respect data structure: CNNs for images, RNNs or transformers for time series, MLPs or ResNets for less structured data, and set transformers for exchangeable datasets of independent observations (Guide, p8:L20-L30; p31:L15-L29).

Large embedding networks need many simulations; transformers can be powerful but may be impractical under small simulation budgets (Guide, p8:L31-L34).

## Mathematical Framework

Represent the raw simulator output

$$
x \in \mathbb{R}^d,\qquad d \gg 1,
$$

by a lower-dimensional summary or embedding

$$
z = S(x) \in \mathbb{R}^r,\qquad r \ll d.
$$

The intended approximation is

$$
p(\theta\mid S(x)) \approx p(\theta\mid x),
$$

meaning that the representation should preserve information about $\theta$ (Guide, p7:L41-L46; p8:L2-L4).

With a learned embedding network $S_\lambda$, neural posterior estimation becomes

$$
q_\phi(\theta\mid S_\lambda(x)) \approx p(\theta\mid x).
$$

The embedding parameters $\lambda$ and posterior-network parameters $\phi$ can be trained jointly using the NPE log-likelihood loss (Guide, p8:L11-L19).

For exchangeable observations $X=\{x_1,\ldots,x_N\}$, the representation should satisfy

$$
S_\lambda(x_1,\ldots,x_N)
=S_\lambda(x_{\pi(1)},\ldots,x_{\pi(N)})
$$

for any permutation $\pi$, matching the permutation invariance of Bayesian inference for conditionally independent datasets (Guide, p8:L26-L30).
