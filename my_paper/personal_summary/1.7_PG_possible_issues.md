# Possible Issues

## Common Flaws And Failure Modes

1. **Observed data outside simulator-prior support.** If $x_o$ lies outside what the simulator and prior can generate, neural SBI may produce unreliable or spurious posteriors instead of simply returning high uncertainty (Guide, p6:L37-L43; p7:L1-L6).

2. **Prior too narrow.** A narrow prior can exclude the true parameter region and create prior misspecification, especially if the observed data requires parameter values outside the chosen bounds (Guide, p6:L37-L43; p7:L7-L10).

3. **Prior too broad.** A broad prior can waste simulations in regions that produce data unlike any observation, increasing cost and reducing accuracy under a fixed simulation budget (Guide, p7:L10-L18).

4. **Insufficient simulations.** Too few simulations can lead to poor inference, overfitting, or bad generalization; required simulation count depends on simulator complexity, data dimension, parameter dimension, and architecture size (Guide, p11:L6-L12; p12:L17-L38; p21:L6-L13).

5. **Bad data representation.** Summary statistics can discard parameter-relevant information, while large learned embeddings may need more simulations than the budget allows (Guide, p8:L2-L10; p8:L31-L34).

6. **Wrong method for the data structure.** NPE is fast and embedding-friendly, but can be simulation-hungry for i.i.d. datasets; NLE/NRE handle i.i.d. observations naturally but need MCMC or VI at inference time (Guide, p9:L14-L28; p31:L41-L49; p32:L1-L7).

7. **Sampling failures after NLE/NRE.** MCMC or VI can fail through poor mixing, bad convergence, or mode collapse, creating incorrect results even if the trained likelihood or ratio network is good (Guide, p12:L4-L15; p30:L32-L37).

8. **Invalid simulator outputs.** Failed simulations can bias NLE/NRE if discarded naively; NPE handles failures more easily by training only on valid pairs, though high failure rates may require an additional success classifier (Guide, p32:L18-L31).

9. **Overconfident networks.** Individual posterior networks can be overconfident; ensembles improved calibration in the neuroscience example (Guide, p20:L5-L16).

10. **Diagnostics are necessary but imperfect.** Global diagnostics can pass even when local errors remain, while local diagnostics are expensive and may themselves be noisy or mis-trained (Guide, p14:L26-L37; p15:L1-L8).

## Mathematical View

The central failure condition is

$$
x_o \notin \operatorname{support}\left(
p_{\text{train}}(x)
\right),
\qquad
p_{\text{train}}(x)=\int p_{\text{sim}}(x\mid\theta)p(\theta)\,d\theta.
$$

Then the learned posterior

$$
q_\phi(\theta\mid x_o)
$$

is an extrapolation outside the simulated joint distribution, not a well-supported approximation to $p(\theta\mid x_o)$ (Guide, p6:L37-L43; p7:L1-L6; p13:L8-L17).

A second broad failure condition is poor calibration:

$$
\mathbb{P}\{\theta^\ast \in C_k(q_\phi(\theta\mid x^\ast))\}\neq k,
$$

meaning that nominal $k\%$ credible regions do not contain the true simulator parameter at the expected rate (Guide, p13:L36-L45; p14:L9-L25; p38:L22-L39).
