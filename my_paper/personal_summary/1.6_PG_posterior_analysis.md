# Posterior Analysis

## Intuition

Posterior analysis begins only after diagnostics suggest the posterior is reliable; the goal is then to use the full distribution to understand uncertainty, dependencies, and plausible parameter explanations (Guide, p15:L10-L15).

Visualizing 1D and 2D marginal or conditional posteriors helps reveal parameter correlations, compensation mechanisms, non-identifiability, and multimodality (Guide, p15:L16-L22).

Broad marginal posteriors do not necessarily mean parameters are unconstrained: conditional posteriors may show that parameters must be finely co-tuned once other parameters are fixed (Guide, p15:L45-L54; p16:L4-L8; p20:L19-L28).

Monte Carlo samples from the posterior can estimate means, variances, covariances, sensitivities, and how strongly different data features constrain different parameters (Guide, p15:L24-L43).

If a single parameter value is needed, the MAP estimate gives the posterior mode, but it should be treated as a summary of a distribution rather than a replacement for uncertainty analysis (Guide, p16:L10-L15).

The posterior can also support decision-making by integrating costs over posterior uncertainty rather than acting on a point estimate (Guide, p16:L17-L26).

## Mathematical Framework

Posterior expectations are estimated by Monte Carlo:

$$
\mathbb{E}_{p(\theta\mid x_o)}[f(\theta)]
=
\int p(\theta\mid x_o)f(\theta)\,d\theta
\approx
\frac{1}{N}\sum_{i=1}^{N} f(\theta_i),
\qquad
\theta_i\sim q_\phi(\theta\mid x_o).
$$

This supports estimates of posterior means, standard deviations, covariances, and other functionals (Guide, p15:L32-L43).

Conditional posterior analysis fixes all but selected parameters:

$$
p(\theta_i\mid \theta_{/i},x_o),
$$

which reveals how much a parameter can vary once the rest of the parameter vector is held fixed (Guide, p15:L45-L54; p16:L1-L8).

A MAP estimate is

$$
\theta_{\text{MAP}}
=
\arg\max_\theta q_\phi(\theta\mid x_o),
$$

and can often be found by optimization when the posterior log-probability is differentiable (Guide, p16:L10-L15).

Bayesian decision theory chooses an action by minimizing posterior expected cost:

$$
a^\star
=
\arg\min_a
\int p(\theta\mid x_o)c(\theta,a)\,d\theta.
$$

This attaches scientific or engineering decisions directly to posterior uncertainty (Guide, p16:L17-L26).
