# Mathematical Framework: BO-Guided Synthetic Simulation for Posterior Inference

## 1. Understanding Checks

Before formalizing the framework, I interpret the brainstorm as follows:

1. The core goal is to learn amortized probabilistic inference for physical systems from synthetic simulator data.

2. The basic inference target is a posterior distribution over latent physical parameters:

   $$
   p(\theta \mid x),
   $$

   where \(\theta\) denotes latent simulator or world parameters and \(x\) denotes observations generated by, or measured from, a physical system.

3. Classical simulation-based inference samples simulator parameters from a prior,

   $$
   \theta \sim p(\theta),
   $$

   generates synthetic observations,

   $$
   x \sim p_{\mathrm{sim}}(x \mid \theta),
   $$

   and trains a neural posterior estimator

   $$
   q_\phi(\theta \mid x) \approx p(\theta \mid x).
   $$

4. The proposed extension is to replace purely random simulation sampling with adaptive simulation design.

5. Bayesian optimization is used as the adaptive mechanism that chooses which simulations should be run next.

6. The BO-selected variables may be latent physical parameters \(\theta\), simulator configuration variables \(\psi\), or both.

7. Simulator configuration variables \(\psi\) represent design choices such as noise, domain randomization, task difficulty, environment diversity, friction ranges, rendering conditions, or simulator fidelity.

8. The simulator therefore becomes

   $$
   x \sim p_{\mathrm{sim}}(x \mid \theta, \psi).
   $$

9. BO is not used to directly solve the physical task. Instead, it is used to improve the synthetic data distribution used to train the posterior network.

10. The main scientific question is whether BO-guided simulation improves posterior quality or simulation efficiency compared with random prior sampling and existing sequential SBI methods.

11. The immediate research prototype should stay modest: benchmark BO-guided SBI on standard SBI tasks such as SLCP, Lotka-Volterra, SIR, or simple physical systems.

12. The long-term ambition is a posterior foundation model for physical systems:

    $$
    q_\phi(\theta \mid x, C),
    $$

    where \(C\) is a context containing task information, simulation history, environment metadata, or examples from related physical systems.

## 2. Objects and Notation

Let

$$
\theta \in \Theta
$$

denote latent physical or simulator parameters.

Examples:

- mass
- friction
- damping
- contact parameters
- hidden environment parameters
- robot dynamics parameters

Let

$$
\psi \in \Psi
$$

denote controllable simulator configuration variables.

Examples:

- noise level
- simulator fidelity
- domain-randomization range
- task difficulty
- environment class
- lighting or sensor model
- perturbation scale

Let

$$
x \in \mathcal{X}
$$

denote an observation, trajectory, time series, image, state-action rollout, or summary statistic generated by the simulator or observed in the real world.

Let

$$
D_t = \{(\theta_i, \psi_i, x_i)\}_{i=1}^t
$$

be the synthetic simulation dataset after \(t\) simulator calls.

The simulator is a conditional stochastic process:

$$
x_i \sim p_{\mathrm{sim}}(x \mid \theta_i, \psi_i).
$$

If no simulator configuration is modeled, this reduces to the classical SBI case:

$$
x_i \sim p_{\mathrm{sim}}(x \mid \theta_i).
$$

## 3. Target Posterior

The ideal target is the posterior under the true data-generating process:

$$
p_{\mathrm{real}}(\theta \mid x)
=
\frac{p_{\mathrm{real}}(x \mid \theta)p(\theta)}
{\int_\Theta p_{\mathrm{real}}(x \mid \theta')p(\theta')\,d\theta'}.
$$

In practice, real likelihoods are unavailable or intractable. SBI therefore trains on simulator samples and learns

$$
q_\phi(\theta \mid x) \approx p_{\mathrm{sim}}(\theta \mid x).
$$

The sim-to-real problem arises because

$$
p_{\mathrm{sim}}(x \mid \theta, \psi) \neq p_{\mathrm{real}}(x \mid \theta)
$$

in general.

One role of \(\psi\) is therefore to control or reduce this mismatch by changing the synthetic data distribution.

## 4. Amortized Posterior Estimation

The posterior network is a conditional density estimator:

$$
q_\phi(\theta \mid x).
$$

For a dataset \(D_t\), a common training objective is negative conditional log likelihood:

$$
\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{NPE}}(\phi; D_t)
=
-\frac{1}{t}\sum_{i=1}^t \log q_\phi(\theta_i \mid x_i).
$$

The trained estimator is

$$
\phi_t^\star
=
\arg\min_\phi \mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{NPE}}(\phi; D_t).
$$

This corresponds to neural posterior estimation. Other SBI estimators could be substituted, such as neural likelihood estimation or neural ratio estimation.

## 5. Adaptive Simulation Design

Instead of sampling all simulations independently from a fixed proposal,

$$
(\theta_i, \psi_i) \sim r(\theta, \psi),
$$

we choose the next simulator input adaptively:

$$
(\theta_{t+1}, \psi_{t+1})
=
\arg\max_{\theta \in \Theta,\, \psi \in \Psi}
a_t(\theta, \psi).
$$

Here \(a_t\) is an acquisition function based on the current dataset \(D_t\), the current posterior estimator \(q_{\phi_t}\), and possibly a surrogate model used by BO.

The simulation loop is:

$$
(\theta_{t+1}, \psi_{t+1})
\xrightarrow{\mathrm{simulator}}
x_{t+1}
\xrightarrow{\mathrm{append}}
D_{t+1}
\xrightarrow{\mathrm{train}}
q_{\phi_{t+1}}.
$$

## 6. Bayesian Optimization Layer

BO requires a scalar objective or utility function

$$
u_t(\theta, \psi)
$$

that measures the usefulness of running a simulation at \((\theta,\psi)\).

Because the true usefulness is expensive or unknown before simulation, BO uses a surrogate model:

$$
g_t(\theta,\psi) \approx u_t(\theta,\psi).
$$

The acquisition function is then constructed from the surrogate posterior:

$$
a_t(\theta,\psi)
=
\alpha\big((\theta,\psi); g_t, D_t\big).
$$

Possible acquisition objectives include:

- expected posterior improvement
- posterior uncertainty reduction
- expected information gain
- disagreement between ensemble posterior estimators
- expected reduction in calibration error
- expected improvement on validation observations
- relevance to a real observation \(x_{\mathrm{real}}\)

## 7. Candidate Utility Functions

### 7.1 Posterior Uncertainty Utility

A simple utility is high posterior uncertainty:

$$
u_t(\theta,\psi)
=
\mathbb{E}_{x \sim p_{\mathrm{sim}}(\cdot \mid \theta,\psi)}
\left[
H(q_{\phi_t}(\theta \mid x))
\right],
$$

where \(H\) denotes entropy.

This favors simulations where the current posterior estimator is uncertain.

### 7.2 Ensemble Disagreement Utility

Train an ensemble of posterior estimators

$$
\{q_{\phi_t^{(m)}}(\theta \mid x)\}_{m=1}^M.
$$

Define utility by predictive disagreement:

$$
u_t(\theta,\psi)
=
\mathbb{E}_{x \sim p_{\mathrm{sim}}(\cdot \mid \theta,\psi)}
\left[
\mathrm{Var}_{m}
\left(
\log q_{\phi_t^{(m)}}(\theta \mid x)
\right)
\right].
$$

This favors simulations where the learned posterior is unstable across models.

### 7.3 Real-Observation Relevance Utility

If a real observation \(x_{\mathrm{real}}\) is available, choose simulations that are likely to improve inference near the real data:

$$
u_t(\theta,\psi)
=
s\left(x_{\mathrm{sim}}(\theta,\psi), x_{\mathrm{real}}\right),
$$

where \(s\) is a similarity or relevance score.

For example:

$$
s(x, x_{\mathrm{real}})
=
-d(f(x), f(x_{\mathrm{real}})),
$$

with feature map \(f\) and distance \(d\).

### 7.4 Calibration Utility

If validation tasks with known parameters are available, define utility by expected reduction in posterior calibration error:

$$
u_t(\theta,\psi)
=
-
\mathrm{ECE}(q_{\phi_{t+1}}),
$$

where \(\mathrm{ECE}\) denotes expected calibration error after adding the candidate simulation.

Because this is expensive to evaluate directly, BO would model it through a surrogate.

## 8. Three Levels of the Framework

### Level 1: BO over Physical Parameters

Only \(\theta\) is optimized:

$$
\theta_{t+1}
=
\arg\max_{\theta \in \Theta}
a_t(\theta).
$$

The simulator is

$$
x \sim p_{\mathrm{sim}}(x \mid \theta).
$$

This is the cleanest starting point for SBI benchmarks.

### Level 2: BO over Simulator Configurations

The physical parameter \(\theta\) is sampled from a prior, while \(\psi\) is optimized:

$$
\theta_t \sim p(\theta),
$$

$$
\psi_{t+1}
=
\arg\max_{\psi \in \Psi}
a_t(\psi).
$$

The simulator is

$$
x \sim p_{\mathrm{sim}}(x \mid \theta, \psi).
$$

This is suitable for domain randomization, noise tuning, or simulator-fidelity selection.

### Level 3: Joint BO over Parameters and Configurations

Both \(\theta\) and \(\psi\) are optimized:

$$
(\theta_{t+1},\psi_{t+1})
=
\arg\max_{\theta \in \Theta,\,\psi \in \Psi}
a_t(\theta,\psi).
$$

This is the most expressive version, but also the hardest because the design space is larger and the selected training distribution may become biased.

## 9. Bias and Corrective Weighting

Adaptive simulation changes the sampling distribution. Instead of drawing from the prior \(p(\theta)\), simulations are drawn from an adaptive proposal:

$$
(\theta_i,\psi_i) \sim r_i(\theta,\psi).
$$

If the posterior estimator should approximate the prior-based posterior, training may require importance weighting.

For parameter-only proposals:

$$
w_i
=
\frac{p(\theta_i)}{r_i(\theta_i)}.
$$

The weighted NPE objective becomes

$$
\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{weighted}}(\phi;D_t)
=
-\frac{1}{t}\sum_{i=1}^t
w_i
\log q_\phi(\theta_i \mid x_i).
$$

This issue is central: BO-guided simulation may improve efficiency, but it must not distort the target posterior unless the distortion is intentional and corrected.

## 10. Foundation-Model Extension

For multiple tasks or environments, define a task variable

$$
\tau \in \mathcal{T}.
$$

Each task has its own parameters, simulator, and context:

$$
\theta_\tau \sim p(\theta \mid \tau),
$$

$$
x_\tau \sim p_{\mathrm{sim}}(x \mid \theta_\tau,\psi_\tau,\tau).
$$

A posterior foundation model conditions on context:

$$
q_\phi(\theta \mid x, C).
$$

The context may contain:

- task descriptors
- simulator metadata
- previous simulation pairs
- real calibration data
- environment descriptions
- robot embodiment information

One possible context structure is

$$
C_\tau
=
\{(\theta_j,\psi_j,x_j)\}_{j=1}^{n_\tau}
\cup
\{\text{metadata}_\tau\}.
$$

The learning objective across tasks is

$$
\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{PFM}}(\phi)
=
-
\mathbb{E}_{\tau \sim p(\tau)}
\mathbb{E}_{(\theta,x,C)\sim p_\tau}
\left[
\log q_\phi(\theta \mid x,C)
\right].
$$

This generalizes from one simulator to a family of physical inference problems.

## 11. Experimental Problem Statement

The first research question can be stated mathematically:

> Given a fixed simulation budget \(B\), does adaptive BO-guided simulation design produce a posterior estimator \(q_{\phi_B}\) with better posterior quality than random or standard sequential SBI simulation policies?

Let \(\pi\) denote a simulation policy:

$$
\pi_t(D_t) \rightarrow (\theta_{t+1}, \psi_{t+1}).
$$

Compare policies:

$$
\pi_{\mathrm{prior}},\quad
\pi_{\mathrm{seqSBI}},\quad
\pi_{\mathrm{BO}}.
$$

For each policy, train

$$
q_{\phi_B^\pi}(\theta \mid x).
$$

Evaluate using a posterior quality metric \(M\):

$$
M(q_{\phi_B^\pi}, p(\theta \mid x)).
$$

The core hypothesis is:

$$
M(q_{\phi_B^{\mathrm{BO}}}, p(\theta \mid x))
>
M(q_{\phi_B^{\mathrm{prior}}}, p(\theta \mid x))
$$

for equal simulation budget \(B\), where larger \(M\) means better posterior quality.

If using error metrics where lower is better, the inequality is reversed.

## 12. Evaluation Metrics

Posterior quality:

- C2ST
- MMD
- KL divergence, when tractable
- simulation-based calibration
- posterior coverage
- posterior log probability on held-out pairs

Simulation efficiency:

- posterior quality as a function of simulator calls
- area under the quality-budget curve
- number of simulations required to reach a target error

Robustness:

- performance under simulator misspecification
- performance under shifted \(\psi\)
- transfer to real or high-fidelity observations

## 13. Minimal Viable Mathematical Prototype

Start with a benchmark simulator:

$$
\theta \sim p(\theta), \qquad x \sim p_{\mathrm{sim}}(x \mid \theta).
$$

Initialize with random simulations:

$$
D_0 = \{(\theta_i,x_i)\}_{i=1}^{n_0}.
$$

For \(t = 0,\dots,B-1\):

1. Train or update \(q_{\phi_t}(\theta \mid x)\) on \(D_t\).
2. Fit a BO surrogate \(g_t(\theta)\) for simulation utility.
3. Select the next parameter:

   $$
   \theta_{t+1}
   =
   \arg\max_{\theta \in \Theta}
   a_t(\theta).
   $$

4. Run the simulator:

   $$
   x_{t+1} \sim p_{\mathrm{sim}}(x \mid \theta_{t+1}).
   $$

5. Update:

   $$
   D_{t+1} = D_t \cup \{(\theta_{t+1},x_{t+1})\}.
   $$

At the end, compare \(q_{\phi_B}\) against baselines under the same budget.

## 14. Key Risks and Open Mathematical Questions

1. Acquisition design: What utility should BO optimize so that it improves posterior quality rather than merely finding unusual simulations?

2. Sampling bias: Does adaptive simulation require importance correction to preserve the desired posterior?

3. High-dimensional design: Can BO scale when \(\theta\), \(\psi\), or \(x\) are high-dimensional?

4. Simulator mismatch: How should \(\psi\) be optimized when \(p_{\mathrm{sim}}\) differs from \(p_{\mathrm{real}}\)?

5. Evaluation: Which posterior quality metrics are reliable when the true posterior is unavailable?

6. Foundation scaling: What context representation \(C\) allows generalization across many physical systems?

## 15. Compact Core Formulation

The project can be summarized as:

$$
\begin{aligned}
(\theta_t,\psi_t) &\sim \pi_t(D_t), \\
x_t &\sim p_{\mathrm{sim}}(x \mid \theta_t,\psi_t), \\
D_{t+1} &= D_t \cup \{(\theta_t,\psi_t,x_t)\}, \\
\phi_{t+1} &=
\arg\min_\phi
\left[
-\sum_{(\theta,\psi,x)\in D_{t+1}}
\log q_\phi(\theta \mid x)
\right],
\end{aligned}
$$

with the BO-guided policy

$$
\pi_t(D_t)
=
\arg\max_{\theta,\psi}
a_t(\theta,\psi;D_t,q_{\phi_t}).
$$

The research claim to test is:

$$
\text{BO-guided } \pi_t
\quad
\Longrightarrow
\quad
\text{better posterior quality per simulator call}.
$$
