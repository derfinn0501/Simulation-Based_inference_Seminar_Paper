# Validation Details

## Intuition

Validation is necessary because the true posterior is usually unavailable, so one cannot directly compare $q_\phi(\theta\mid x_o)$ to $p(\theta\mid x_o)$ for real observations (Guide, p12:L40-L45; p13:L1-L6).

Misspecification checks ask whether the observed data $x_o$ lies inside the distribution generated by the simulator and prior; these checks can often be performed before training the inference network (Guide, p13:L8-L20).

Posterior predictive checks sample parameters from the estimated posterior, rerun the simulator, and compare the simulated outputs to $x_o$; failure suggests either too little training data or model misspecification (Guide, p13:L22-L34).

Coverage diagnostics test calibration: a posterior should not be systematically too narrow, too broad, or skewed, and the true generating parameters should fall in credible regions at the expected rate (Guide, p13:L36-L45; p14:L9-L25).

Global diagnostics are cheaper and average over many observations, but passing them is only a necessary condition; local diagnostics are more specific to $x_o$ but require more simulations and may introduce their own diagnostic-model errors (Guide, p14:L26-L37; p15:L1-L8).

## Mathematical Framework

For misspecification, estimate whether

$$
x_o \sim p_{\text{train}}(x)
=\int p_{\text{sim}}(x\mid\theta)p(\theta)\,d\theta.
$$

A low density or out-of-distribution $x_o$ indicates that posterior inference is being asked outside the simulator-prior support (Guide, p13:L8-L20).

For posterior predictive checks:

$$
\theta^{(m)}\sim q_\phi(\theta\mid x_o),
\qquad
\tilde{x}^{(m)}\sim p_{\text{sim}}(x\mid \theta^{(m)}).
$$

Then compare $\{\tilde{x}^{(m)}\}$ to $x_o$, often alongside prior predictives (Guide, p13:L22-L34).

For global calibration, draw a calibration set

$$
\theta_i^\ast\sim p(\theta),\qquad
x_i^\ast\sim p_{\text{sim}}(x\mid\theta_i^\ast),
$$

infer $q_\phi(\theta\mid x_i^\ast)$, and compare each posterior to the known generating parameter $\theta_i^\ast$ (Guide, p14:L9-L25).

Expected coverage checks whether the ground-truth parameter falls inside nominal $k\%$ posterior regions at frequency $k\%$; SBC checks marginal rank uniformity; TARP uses random-point credible regions and can work without density evaluation (Guide, p14:L15-L25; p38:L19-L39).

Local diagnostics condition calibration on the observation space, e.g.

$$
\operatorname{coverage}(x)
\approx
\mathbb{P}\left(\theta^\ast \in C_k(q_\phi(\theta\mid x))\mid x\right),
$$

so that a specific observation $x_o$ can be diagnosed rather than only the average behavior across observations (Guide, p38:L41-L52; p39:L1-L13).
