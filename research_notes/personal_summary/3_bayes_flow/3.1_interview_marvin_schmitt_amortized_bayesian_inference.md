# Interview With Marvin Schmitt: Amortized Bayesian Inference

## Current Thesis

Neural SBI gives two related but distinct advantages:

1. likelihood-free posterior learning with flexible neural density estimators;
2. amortized inference across many observations.

The bridge between them is important: amortization is not the only reason to use
neural SBI. The core reason is that neural networks can represent complex
posteriors when the likelihood is unavailable or intractable. Amortization is an
additional advantage when the learned posterior estimator can be reused.

## 1. SBI, Neural Posteriors, and Amortization

SBI is useful when the likelihood $p(x \mid \theta)$ is unavailable,
intractable, or too expensive to evaluate, but simulation from the model is
possible. Neural posterior estimation learns a conditional approximation

$$
q_\phi(\theta \mid x)
$$

from simulated pairs $(\theta, x)$.

Neural networks are a natural tool here because they can approximate flexible
conditional distributions, including complex, skewed, or multimodal posteriors.
Amortization enters when the network is trained across many possible
observations $x$, rather than only for one fixed observation $x_o$. After that
expensive training phase, the same network can be reused for fast posterior
inference on new observations.

The key distinction is:

- neural SBI gives flexible likelihood-free posterior approximation;
- amortized Bayesian inference gives repeated-use inference after training.

## 2. When Amortized Inference Pays Off

Amortized Bayesian inference is most sensible when there are many related
inference problems generated by the same model. Each dataset or entity has its
own observation $x_i$ and its own unknown parameters $\theta_i$, but the
simulator, prior assumptions, and inference target stay structurally similar.

This setting appears in repeated experimental runs, patients, galaxies,
subjects, devices, or simulated systems: many versions of the same kind of
problem, each requiring a posterior over its own parameters.

The tradeoff is computational. Training the neural posterior estimator can be
slow and simulation-heavy, but inference after training is fast. MCMC or another
targeted method may be competitive for a single entity when the likelihood is
available or when the inference is focused on one observation. Across many
entities, however, repeating a case-specific method can become expensive.
Amortization becomes attractive when the upfront training cost can be shared
across many later inference calls.

## 3. The Bridge to the Main Argument

The core idea of neural SBI is not amortization itself. The core idea is to
learn a powerful posterior approximation without requiring likelihood
evaluation. The neural network is valuable because it can represent posterior
shapes that are difficult for analytic approximations or simple parametric
families.

Amortization is an additional feature of this setup. It becomes especially
useful when:

- many observations or entities share the same simulator and prior;
- the model is stable enough that the training distribution remains relevant;
- inference must be repeated often for new observations;
- fast posterior inference is needed after training;
- simulated training data can be reused across future inference tasks.

The caveat is that full amortization may be wasteful if only one observation
matters. In that case, an observation-focused or sequential SBI method may use
the simulation budget more efficiently because it concentrates effort near the
observed data instead of learning the whole prior-predictive space.

One-sentence takeaway:

> Neural SBI is mainly about flexible likelihood-free posterior learning;
> amortization is the payoff when the same inference problem repeats many times.
